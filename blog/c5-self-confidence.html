What is the relationship between mindfulness and self awareness?

Mindfulness and self awareness are two emotional competencies that may present themselves as the same thing, but are actually quite distinct with their own purposes. It's when they are used together that they enable the most compelling and powerful emotional competency: self assessment.

Mindfulness is the practice of looking through your mind, your body, and your emotions objectively, observing, defining and accepting your psychological, physiological and emotional states. If you were to consider your whole being as a system, you can consider your everyday happenings and experiences as, "Inputs," or even, "Arguments". With mindfulness, you can observe what happens to your system when it is presented with an argument. With that observation as your baseline, you can train or program your own system to a new state of being.

Self awareness, for lack of better expression, is same but different. While mindfulness analyses the whole of the being deeply and extensively, self awareness is the constant monitoring in real-time, keeping yourself grounded and focused. It's hardening your being for the unpredictability of everyday, constantly querying whether your responses or actions are acceptable or appropriate. If mindfulness is about defining your functions (your responses to arguments or inputs), self awareness is parsing those arguments into those well-trained functions.

Hence, the relationship between mindfulness and self awareness is that they are distinct emotional competencies that share the property of being trainable and practical. They are applied deeply and broadly, and they work in tandem to fulfil self assessment. The core relationship built when both are used together is that one can build a repository of functions, analyse these functions' responses to arguments, and train these functions towards an improved end state. Repeating this process is continuous improvement, and response to the dynamic unpredictability of the world around us: by harnessing this relationship wisely, we are ready for anything.

Try at least one of the self awareness practices from the chapter (Body Scan, Journaling). What was your experience of it? How did you feel?

Today I tried Body Scanning after reading the self awareness practices from this chapter. Lying down, I brought my mind into a neutral state and made observations about my physiological state. I found I had a bit of a headache, and found it was above my left eye. I felt well accustomed and well-trained in body scanning in its neutral state: I have listened extensively to mindfulness audio tracks to help me sleep deeply.

To observe the physiological effects of my body in a happy state, I recalled some of my happiest memories: those spent with my mother as a small child. The difference was utterly profound. I felt an elevated sense of awareness in my hands, on top of my hands and arms, elevated awareness on the bottom of my feet, and my legs were ready to have a "spring" in my step. I imagined myself by my mum's side, laughing and walking and exploring the world with her. I felt safe, energised, and eager to see what was next. I felt my breath shorten and sharpen, ready for me to bound ahead. It was warm, and I was happy. My mum left this world in February 2016, but I certainly felt her presence during that mindful moment.

The self awareness practice was exhilarating, but frightening at the same time. I didn't realise just how much emotions affected the body until this experiment. From this, I have lot to reflect upon over the next few weeks.

What did you take away from the "My emotions are not me" section?

I found this section grounding and empowering. It brought emotions which are in a fraught state of ambiguity and subjectivity into a neutral, objective state. Growing up, my dad always gave me the advice, "Separate your concerns." If you can distance who you are from your emotions, you can analyse things laid out in front of you carefully and calmly, without interference of emotions clouding your judgment. It's also a powerful mechanism to redirect people from criticising you over your emotional state - i.e. depersonalising arguments into problem solving collaboratively and efficiently. This helps me continue to be an optimistic person: assume positive intent from all. We all want to solve the same problems for the best outcome together. Let's do it with enthusiasm and optimism.
