What a responsive site is, and why responsiveness is important

A responsive site is a web page or web site that has been programmed to:
  • Query the device and/or resolution capacity of the user's browser
  • Review the assets and content it is presenting to the user, and
  • Render the assets and content it is preesnting to the user based on the styling and the resolution capacity of the user's browser.

Another way to put it is that the website makes sure the the user experiences the website's content, design, and overall experience regardless of the device size they are using to read the site. Images too big for the screen? Resize them! Heading taking too much real estate on the screen? Resize that too!

Responsiveness is important, as we want the user to experience our website and its content no matter the device they are using to reach our website. Rather than maintaining several source code repositories that are bespoke on a per-screen-type-basis, a well-built responsive website is one that is coded to render to any screen resolution from the one source code repository.

What mobile-first design is, and why it's important.

A mobile-first design is a website that has its default rendering behaviour as that on the smallest screen from the device range: mobile phones. Good design and software delivery assumes that the user has limited screen size, and then scales up to larger screens.

Mobile-first design is important for a number of reason:
  • If we as web developers assume that the user has the largest screen available, we end up making our design decisions based on a limited subset of users.
  • We have reached the point in technology where mobile users now exceed desktop users.
  • This is an incredible statistic: the demand for responsive designs will only increase from here, and should be considered best practice.
  • We want our websites to work for everyone.

What frameworks are, and their pros and cons.

Frameworks are code bases which have been built by other developers. They are built to demonstrate best-practice to other developers through the principle of, Show, don't tell.

The pros of frameworks are:
  • In the case of open-source frameworks, they are free to use, and you even have the opportunity to contribute to the project.
  • A well-built framework doesn't bring function-breaking issues or questionable design decisions.
  • They help the developer deliver at speed with solid foundations: the developer does not have to build their HTML or CSS from scratch.
The cons of frameworks are:
  • They take a while to get familiar with them for the first time.
  • Learning how the framework works depends on a lot of trial and error and exploration.
  • If the framework or any of its dependencies are redundant or no longer supported, it can be a nuisance to migrate to another framework.

What a wireframe is, and why we use it.

Wireframes are content-agnostic ways of designing the structure of your website. IT enables web developers and designers alike to collaborate, assess feasibility, and agree on the execution of the structure of a website without writing a single line of code. This lets everyone involved in the website know what to expect in general from the structure of the site: no assumptions, no surprises.

Wireframes also help developers organise their code around their website structure, and predict how many columns and rows are going to be needed for their grid-based design.
